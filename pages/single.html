Wei Liang

Story

15

In a small Malaysian town, nestled among the vibrant streets of night markets and coffee shops, lived the Lim family. Mr. and Mrs. Lim were proud parents of their 14-year-old son, Wei Liang, a boy with autism.

Wei Liang saw the world in his own unique way. He was captivated by patterns—whether it was the way raindrops slid down a window or the symmetrical tiles on a shop floor. He often struggled with loud noises and unexpected changes, but he had a talent for drawing intricate landscapes that seemed to come alive on paper.

For years, the Lims felt isolated. Many in their community didn’t understand autism and mistook Wei Liang’s behaviors for rudeness or lack of discipline. The family avoided crowded places, fearing judgment when Wei Liang became overwhelmed.

Support from AutiReach

One day, Mrs. Lim stumbled upon AutiReach, an initiative designed to connect the autism community with society. Its platform offered resources for caregivers, awareness programs for the public, and events tailored for individuals with autism. Intrigued, she signed up for a seminar.

At the seminar, Mrs. Lim met other parents who shared similar struggles. A keynote speaker, Dr. Norzila Zakaria, shared insights on how to support children with autism. “Autism is not a barrier,” she said passionately. “It’s a different way of experiencing the world. With the right support, these children can thrive.”

Encouraged, Mrs. Lim enrolled Wei Liang in a drawing workshop hosted by AutiReach. The workshop wasn’t just for children with autism—it was open to the community, creating a space for inclusion.

Community gap

At first, Wei Liang was hesitant. He sat in a corner, clutching his sketchbook. Slowly, a curious boy named Arif approached him.“Can I see your drawings?” he asked. Wei Liang didn’t respond but opened his sketchbook to a page filled with a stunning drawing of a rainforest.

Arif’s eyes widened. “This is amazing! Can you teach me?”

It was a simple question, but it sparked something in Wei Liang. For the first time, he wasn’t just the boy who struggled in noisy classrooms—he was an artist, appreciated for his talent.The workshop became a turning point. Wei Liang began participating in more AutiReach programs. The platform also offered a Motivation Program, where he could access mentorship sessions, including ones that taught him how to sell his artwork online through their eCommerce module.

Meanwhile, AutiReach’s awareness campaigns started changing the community’s perspective. Schools introduced inclusive education strategies, local businesses began hiring individuals with autism, and families like the Lims felt less alone. One evening, Wei Liang’s artwork was displayed at a local art exhibition organized by AutiReach. The event was attended by people from all walks of life, including government representatives and community leaders. Standing by his artwork, Wei Liang received compliments from strangers who admired his talent. Mr. Lim, watching from a distance, felt a deep sense of pride. “This is what inclusion looks like,” he whispered to Mrs. Lim. “Wei Liang is no longer hidden. He’s a part of this community.” AutiReach became more than just a platform—it was a bridge connecting individuals with autism to the broader society. It gave families like the Lims hope and helped others see the beauty in neurodiversity.

Why We Need AutiReach

AutiReach bridges the gap between the autism community and society, fostering understanding, inclusion, and empowerment. It provides resources for caregivers, awareness programs to reduce stigma, and platforms for individuals with autism to showcase their talents.

By creating a supportive community, AutiReach helps break barriers, ensuring everyone has a chance to thrive.
